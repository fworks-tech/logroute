## What Are the Hours of Service (HOS) Regulations?

The HOS regulations are found in **49 CFR Part 395** of the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Regulations (FMCSRs).

- They are developed and enforced by the **Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA)**
- They apply to property-carrying CMVs operating in interstate commerce
- This guide is informational only and does not replace the actual regulations

---

## Who Must Comply?

Unless an exception applies, you must follow the HOS regulations if you drive a CMV used on highways in interstate commerce to transport property and:

- Weighs **10,001 pounds (4,536 kg) or more**, including load; or
- Has a **gross vehicle weight rating (GVWR)** or **gross combination weight rating (GCWR)** of **10,001 pounds or more**; or
- Is transporting hazardous materials in a quantity requiring placards

---

## Interstate vs. Intrastate Commerce

### Interstate commerce

Interstate commerce means trade, traffic, or transportation in the United States:

- Between a place in a State and a place outside that State
- Between two places in a State through another State or outside the United States
- Between two places in a State as part of trade originating or terminating outside the State or the United States

If you have just delivered an interstate load and are driving empty to pick up another interstate load, the truck is still considered property in the stream of interstate commerce.

At the point you start driving in interstate commerce, you must have:

- A signed statement, or
- Records of duty status

showing your total time on duty for the preceding **7 days**.

You generally must also follow the Federal HOS rules for a period after you stop operating in interstate commerce:

- **60-hour/7-day schedule:** follow Federal HOS for the next **7 days**
- **70-hour/8-day schedule:** follow Federal HOS for the next **8 days**

### Intrastate commerce

Intrastate commerce means transportation not covered by the interstate definition. Usually, that means the cargo stays within a single State.

If you operate only in intrastate commerce, the Federal HOS regulations generally do not apply, but most States have similar rules.

## Personal Use of a CMV, Personal Conveyance, and Yard Moves

### Personal conveyance

Personal use, often called **personal conveyance**, is the movement of a CMV for personal use while off duty.

Examples:

- Driving from a truck stop to a restaurant
- Using the vehicle to transport your own possessions

A driver may record personal conveyance as **off duty** only when the driver is relieved from all work and responsibility by the motor carrier.

The CMV may be used for personal conveyance even if it is laden, as long as the load is not being transported for the commercial benefit of the motor carrier at that time.

### Yard moves

Yard moves are transfers of CMVs between locations within a terminal or similar facility on private property.

- Yard moves may not occur on a highway
- Under ELD rules, yard moves are classified as **on-duty not driving** time

## What Is On-Duty Time?

On-duty time includes all time you are working, or required to be ready to work, for any employer.

It includes:

- Time at a plant, terminal, facility, or other property of a motor carrier or shipper, or on public property, waiting to be dispatched, unless relieved from duty
- Time inspecting, servicing, conditioning, fueling, or washing a truck
- All driving time
- All other time in or on a CMV except:
  - Time resting in or on a parked vehicle, except as otherwise provided in § 397.5
  - Time resting in a sleeper berth
  - Up to 3 hours riding in the passenger seat of a property-carrying vehicle moving on the highway immediately before or after at least 7 consecutive hours in the sleeper berth
- Time loading, unloading, supervising, attending the truck, or handling paperwork for shipments
- Time caring for a broken-down truck
- Time providing a breath, saliva, or urine sample for drug/alcohol testing, including travel to and from the collection site
- Time doing any other work for a motor carrier, including training and driving a company car
- Time doing paid work for someone who is not a motor carrier

The bottom line: on-duty time includes all time you are working for a motor carrier, whether paid or not, and all paid work for anyone else.

---

## What Is Off-Duty Time?

To be off duty, you must:

- Be relieved of all duty and responsibility for performing work
- Be free to pursue activities of your own choosing
- Be able to leave the place where your vehicle is parked

If you are not doing any work for a motor carrier and not doing paid work for anyone else, you may record the time as off duty.

## What Are the Hours of Service Limits?

The HOS regulations focus on fatigue by placing specific limits on:

- How long you may drive
- How long you may work
- How much total on-duty time you may accumulate

The three core limits are:

- **14-hour driving window**
- **11-hour driving limit**
- **60-hour/7-day or 70-hour/8-day on-duty limit**

---

### 14-Hour Driving Window

**49 CFR § 395.3(a)(2)**

- You may drive within a **14 consecutive-hour window**
- That window begins when you start **any kind of work**
- You may drive up to 11 hours inside that window, after **10 or more consecutive hours off duty**
- Once the 14-hour window ends, you cannot drive again until you have had another 10 consecutive hours off duty, or the equivalent

Example:

- You come to work at **6:00 a.m.** after 10 consecutive hours off
- You must not drive after **8:00 p.m.** that same day

---

### 11-Hour Driving Limit

**49 CFR § 395.3(a)(3)**

- Within the 14-hour window, you may drive for no more than **11 total hours**
- Driving is not permitted if more than **8 hours** have passed since the end of the driver’s last break in driving time of at least **30 minutes**
- Once you reach 11 hours of driving, you must be off duty for 10 consecutive hours, or the equivalent, before driving again

Example:

- Start work at **6:00 a.m.**
- Drive from **7:00 a.m. to 2:00 p.m.** = 7 hours
- Take a 30-minute break
- Drive another 4 hours until **6:30 p.m.**
- You must not drive again until you have at least 10 consecutive hours off duty

---

### Sleeper Berth Provision

**49 CFR § 395.1(g)**

If you drive a truck with a sleeper berth that meets the definition in § 395.2, you may use it in three ways:

1. **10 consecutive hours in the sleeper berth**
   - Fully restarts the 11-hour driving limit and 14-hour duty period

2. **At least 7 consecutive hours in the sleeper berth + up to 3 hours off duty or as a passenger**
   - The two periods must total at least 10 consecutive hours

3. **Split sleeper berth**
   - One qualifying period of at least **7 consecutive hours** in the sleeper berth
   - Paired with another qualifying period of at least **2 hours** in the sleeper berth or off duty
   - Together they must total at least **10 hours**
   - Neither paired period counts against the 14-hour window

#### Key calculation rules

- The calculation starts at the **end of the first qualifying rest period**
- The calculation ends at the **start of the second qualifying rest period**
- Only the paired qualifying rest periods are excluded from the 14-hour driving window
- If you take a later qualifying rest period, the calculation point moves forward to the end of the new first qualifying rest period

---

### 30-Minute Rest Break

**49 CFR § 395.3(a)(3)(ii)**

- Required after **8 cumulative driving hours**
- The 8 hours are cumulative, not consecutive
- The break must be **30 consecutive minutes**
- It may be taken:
  - On duty
  - Off duty
  - In the sleeper berth

Important details:

- Two non-consecutive periods cannot be combined
- A driver could, for example, use 15 minutes on-duty not driving plus 15 minutes off duty if the periods are consecutive
- The 30-minute break does **not** extend the 14-hour window or increase maximum driving time
- It does **not** apply to drivers qualifying for the short-haul exceptions in § 395.1(e)(1) or § 395.1(e)(2)

---

### 60/70-Hour On-Duty Limit

**49 CFR § 395.3(b)**

This is a rolling limit based on a **7- or 8-day period**, not a fixed calendar week.

| Schedule | Limit | Period |
|---|---:|---|
| 60-hour/7-day | 60 hours | Any 7 consecutive days |
| 70-hour/8-day | 70 hours | Any 8 consecutive days |

Rules:

- Use **60/7** if your company does not operate vehicles every day of the week
- Use **70/8** if your company does operate every day of the week
- Once you reach the limit, you may continue on duty, but you may not drive
- The oldest day drops off each day as you recalculate the rolling total

Example rolling total:

| Day | Hours |
|---|---:|
| Sunday | 0 |
| Monday | 10 |
| Tuesday | 8.5 |
| Wednesday | 12.5 |
| Thursday | 9 |
| Friday | 10 |
| Saturday | 12 |
| Sunday | 5 |

- **Days 1–8:** 67 hours
- **Days 2–9:** 73 hours
- **Days 3–10:** 63 hours

---

### 34-Hour Restart

**49 CFR §§ 395.3(c)(1) and (c)(2)**

- Taking **34 or more consecutive hours off duty**, in the sleeper berth, or a combination of both resets the 60- or 70-hour weekly clock to zero
- This is optional, not mandatory

## Exceptions

### Adverse Driving Conditions

**49 CFR § 395.1(b)(1)** and definition in § 395.2

If unexpected adverse driving conditions slow you down:

- You may drive up to **2 additional hours**
- You may extend the 14-hour window by up to **2 hours**
- This can bring total driving time to **13 hours**

The condition must be:

- Unknown to the driver before the start of the duty day, or
- Not reasonably knowable before dispatch or before resuming after a qualifying sleeper berth rest break

Examples:

- Highway blocked by a crash
- Sudden fog

Not included:

- Typical rush-hour congestion

ELD users must annotate the adverse condition on the record of duty status.

---

### CDL Short-Haul Exception

**49 CFR § 395.1(e)(1)**

To qualify, the driver must:

- Return to the normal work reporting location and be released from work within **14 consecutive hours**
- Operate within a **150 air-mile radius** of the normal work reporting location
- Have at least **10 consecutive hours off duty** between shifts

Additional points:

- The **11-hour driving limit still applies**
- The driver is exempt from § 395.8 and § 395.11
- No logbook or ELD is required if the time record exception is used
- The 30-minute break does not apply

The carrier must retain time records for **6 months** showing:

- Time the driver reports for duty
- Total hours on duty each day
- Time the driver is released each day
- Total time for the preceding 7 days for drivers used for the first time or intermittently

---

### Non-CDL Short-Haul Exception

**49 CFR § 395.1(e)(2)**

This applies if:

- The vehicle does not require a CDL
- The driver returns to the normal work reporting location every day
- The operation is within a **150 air-mile radius**

Limits:

- No driving past the **14th hour** after coming on duty for more than **5 days** in any 7-consecutive-day period
- No driving past the **16th hour** after coming on duty for more than **2 days** in any 7-consecutive-day period

Additional points:

- No logbook required if the time record exception is used
- Carrier must retain time records for 6 months
- Drivers using this exception are not eligible for:
  - The CDL short-haul exception
  - The 16-hour short-haul exception
  - The split sleeper berth provision

---

### 16-Hour Short-Haul Exception

**49 CFR § 395.1(o)**

- Extends the 14-hour driving window to **16 hours**
- May be used **once every 7 consecutive days** or after a **34-hour restart**
- Driver must return to the work reporting location that day and for the last 5 duty tours
- Driver must be released within **16 consecutive hours** of coming on duty
- Not available to drivers using the non-CDL short-haul exception

## Driver’s Daily Log and Electronic Logging Device (ELD)

Most motor carriers and drivers required to maintain records of duty status under **§ 395.8(a)** must use an **ELD**.

More information:

- ELD information: https://eld.fmcsa.dot.gov

Paper logs may be allowed when:

- RODS are required on **8 or fewer days** within the last 30 days
- The operation is a driveaway-towaway operation
- The vehicle is manufactured before **model year 2000**

---

## What Must the Record of Duty Status Include?

Each RODS page must include a 24-hour graph grid and the following information:

| Field | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Date | Month, day, and year for the beginning of each 24-hour period |
| Total miles driven today | Total miles driven during the 24-hour period |
| Truck or tractor and trailer number | Company unit number(s), or license number and state |
| Name of carrier | Name of the motor carrier(s) worked for |
| Main office address | City and state are sufficient |
| Driver’s signature | Legal name or name of record certifying accuracy |
| Name of co-driver | If applicable |
| Time base | Time zone in effect at the home terminal |
| Remarks | City/town/village and state abbreviation at each duty status change |
| Total hours | Total hours for each duty status; entries must total 24 hours |
| Shipping documents | Shipping document number, or shipper name and commodity |

---

## The Graph Grid

The graph grid must show:

- Off Duty
- Sleeper Berth
- Driving
- On Duty (Not Driving)

Rules:

- The grid must be kept current to the last change of duty status
- If an inspector stops you, the logbook should show the time and place of your last duty status change
- Each status is shown with a solid line across the appropriate time markers

---

## The Remarks

Each time you change duty status, you must record the city, town, or village and the state abbreviation in the Remarks section.

If the location is not in a city, town, or village, use one of these:

- Highway number and nearest milepost, plus nearest city/town/village and state
- Highway number and service plaza name, plus nearest city/town/village and state
- Highways of the two nearest intersecting roadways, plus nearest city/town/village and state

You may also use Remarks for:

- Shipping information
- Adverse driving conditions
- Crossing a State line

---

## A Completed Grid

A sample log in the guide shows a driver who:

- Reported for work at **6:00 a.m.**
- Loaded freight and checked with dispatch
- Completed a pre-trip inspection
- Drove from Richmond, VA to Newark, NJ
- Took fuel, lunch, delivery, and sleeper berth breaks
- Went off duty at **9:00 p.m.**

Key point:

- The driver was on duty past the 14th hour, but that is allowed as long as there is **no CMV driving after the 14th hour**

---

## A Completed Log

After you complete your log:

- You have **13 days** to submit the original copy to your carrier
- You must keep a copy of the completed log for the next **7 consecutive days**
- The copies must be available for inspection by law enforcement

If you work for more than one motor carrier during a 24-hour period, you must provide copies to each carrier and include:

- All duty time for the entire 24-hour period
- The name of each motor carrier
- The beginning and finishing times for each carrier
